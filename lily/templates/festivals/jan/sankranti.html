MAKARA SANKRANTI

Makar Sankranti marks the transition of the Sun into Makara RÃ¢shi (Capricorn) on its celestial path, and the beginning of the six-month Uttarayana period. This festival is celebrated across India with different names and traditions, but universally signifies the end of winter and the start of longer days.

Makar Sankranti is unique as it's one of the few Hindu festivals based on the solar calendar rather than the lunar calendar, which is why it falls on approximately the same date every year (January 14 or 15). The festival celebrates the harvest season and is associated with special foods made of sesame and jaggery, kite flying, bonfires, and ritual baths in holy rivers.

In different regions, the festival is known as Pongal (Tamil Nadu), Lohri (Punjab), Bihu (Assam), and Uttarayan (Gujarat). The festival symbolizes the victory of light over darkness and knowledge over ignorance, marking an important astronomical event when the sun begins its northward journey.
